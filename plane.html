Flying with Your Dog: Everything You Need to Know

Planning to take your dog on a plane? While most healthy adult dogs can handle flying just fine, there are many things to consider before booking that ticket. From choosing the right flight to making sure your pup is comfortable in a travel crate, proper preparation is key. Below, we’ve gathered the most important tips to ensure a safe and stress-free journey for both you and your furry companion.

Is Your Dog Fit to Fly?

Not every dog is suited for air travel. Before making any plans, always consult your vet to ensure your pup is fit for flying. Certain breeds and conditions make air travel riskier, so it’s important to assess whether flying is the best option for your dog.

  • Brachycephalic (short-nosed) breeds like Bulldogs and Pugs may have breathing difficulties at high altitudes, making air travel unsafe for them.
  • Young puppies, elderly dogs, pregnant dogs, and sick dogs should avoid flying due to potential health risks.
  • Nervous or anxious dogs may struggle with the unfamiliar experience of flying, so consider their temperament carefully.
  • Still unsure? A visit to the vet can help determine if your dog is ready for takeoff.

Is Your Dog Allowed to Fly?

Not all airlines accept pets, especially budget airlines. Before booking, check whether your dog is even allowed on board.

  • Many low-cost airlines do not allow pets, so always verify their policy first.
  • Each airline has different rules, including breed restrictions, carrier size limits, and health requirements.
  • Check your airline’s pet policy in advance to avoid surprises at the airport.

Choosing the Right Flight for Your Dog

Just like humans, dogs prefer a smooth and quick journey. Choosing the right flight can make a huge difference in your dog’s travel experience.

  • Opt for direct flights whenever possible—this reduces stress and eliminates the risks associated with layovers.
  • Book early morning or late evening flights to avoid crowds and ensure a calmer experience.
  • Arrive at the airport early—at least two extra hours—to allow time for check-in and security procedures.
  • Call the airline in advance to notify them that you’ll be traveling with a dog and confirm their pet policies.
  • Check pet capacity limits—some airlines only allow a limited number of dogs per flight, so reserve your pet’s spot early.
  • Confirm your booking 1-2 days before departure to ensure your dog’s travel arrangements are still in place.

    • How Will Your Dog Travel?

    There are three main ways for dogs to fly, depending on their size and airline regulations.

    • In the cabin: Small dogs that fit in an airline-approved carrier can travel under the seat in front of you. Your dog must stay inside the carrier throughout the flight.
    • In the cargo hold: Larger dogs must travel in a well-ventilated crate in the plane’s cargo area. Airlines have strict regulations on crate size and design.
    • As cargo (freight): Some dogs are transported separately from their owners as cargo, which can be a cheaper but less convenient option.

    How Much Does It Cost to Fly with a Dog?

    The price of flying with your dog varies by airline and travel method.

  • Cabin or cargo hold: Usually charged as excess baggage, based on the combined weight of your dog and their crate.
  • Cargo transport: Typically follows freight pricing, which may be lower but comes with separate handling.
  • Always check with your airline for their specific pet travel fees before booking.

    • Preparing Your Dog for Travel: Crate Training

    Since your dog will spend the entire flight in a travel crate, it’s important to get them comfortable with it in advance.

  • Introduce the crate early—place it in your home and let your dog explore it freely.
  • Use treats and toys to create a positive association with the crate.
  • Practice closing the crate door while you’re nearby, gradually increasing the time your dog spends inside.
  • Ensure your dog can rest calmly inside before travel day arrives.

    • Checklist: Flying in the Cargo Hold

    If your dog will be traveling in the cargo hold, make sure their crate meets airline requirements and is set up properly.

  • A well-ventilated crate that can be securely closed without locks.
  • Absorbent material (like blankets or newspapers) to keep the crate dry.
  • The right size—your dog should be able to stand, lie down, and turn around comfortably.

    • Food and Water: What’s Best Before a Flight?

    To avoid discomfort, manage your dog’s food and water intake before the flight.

  • No food six hours before departure to prevent motion sickness.
  • Limit water intake three hours before flying to avoid accidents.
  • Consult your vet for specific dietary recommendations before travel.

    • Should You Give Your Dog Sedatives?

    Calming medication might seem like a good idea, but in most cases, it’s not recommended.

  • Sedatives can lower blood pressure and body temperature, increasing health risks.
  • Drowsy dogs may have difficulty breathing at high altitudes.
  • Some airlines won’t allow sedated pets on board.
  • Try natural calming aids or vet-approved alternatives instead.

    • Check-In, Delays and Arrival, What to Expect

    The airport process can be stressful, so plan ahead to keep things running smoothly.

  • Find the special check-in counter for pets (ask your airline where it is).
  • Exercise your dog before heading to the airport—let them burn off energy!
  • Have all required paperwork ready at check-in.
  • During delays, notify the airline that your dog is in the cargo hold and request temperature checks.
  • If you have a layover, pick up your dog and re-check them onto the next flight—never assume they’ll be transferred automatically.
  • Upon arrival, ask for priority unloading so your pup doesn’t stay in the cargo area longer than necessary.

    • Still Unsure? Consider Alternatives

    Flying isn’t the best option for every dog. If you’re unsure about your dog’s ability to handle air travel, you might want to leave them at home.

  • Consider a trusted pet sitter or dog boarding service.
  • If possible, opt for a road trip instead—it’s often a less stressful alternative!
